Background:Training volume is a key variable in resistance training.However, the impact of training volume in flywheel training adaptations has not been thoroughly explored, particularly regarding muscle strength, power, and hypertrophy, as well as performance of athletic tasks such as jumping, sprinting, and change of direction (COD).Objective: This systematic review aimed to determine the long-term dose-response relationships in flywheel resistance training and provide a robust synthesis of its effects on physical performance. Methods: Following PRISMA guidelines, four databases were searched for longitudinal studies implementing 4 weeks of flywheel training and reporting pre-to post-change in overall or eccentric strength, concentric or eccentric power, hypertrophy, jump, sprint, or COD performance.Multilevel meta-analyses were conducted to estimate the overall training effects and investigate the relationship between training volume and the magnitude of adaptations.Results: Fifty-five studies were included in data synthesis.Large improvements were found in concentric and eccentric power (SMC = 0.943 and SMC = 1.058, p < 0.01, respectively).Moderate increases were observed in overall and eccentric strength (SMC = 0.664 and SMC = 0.712, p < 0.01, respectively), while hypertrophy, jump, sprint, and COD showed small improvements (SMC = 0.348, SMC = 0.367, SMC = 0.323, and SMC = 0.463, respectively).A dose-response relationship was observed for hypertrophy ( = 0.016 [0.003, 0.029], p = 0.018).Higher inertias improved eccentric power more than lower inertias ( = 50.926[1.467, 100.386], p = 0.044), though a negative interaction was observed between weekly training volume and inertia ( = -0.727[-1.437, -0.017], p = 0.045).Bilateral exercises improved overall strength more than unilateral or mixed-limb exercises ( = -0.674[-0.949, -0.400], p < 0.001; = -0.571[-0.945, -0.198], p = 0.004, respectively).Concentric power improvements were greater in highly trained athletes compared to trained athletes ( = -1.858[-3.567, -0.148], p = 0.034).Flywheel-specific power assessment methods showed a significant difference compared to traditional methods ( = -0.918[-1.435, -0.401], p < .001).No dose-response relationships were found for overall and eccentric strength, concentric power, jump, sprint, or COD performance (p > 0.05). Conclusions:Flywheel training is particularly effective for improving muscle strength and power.A doseresponse relationship was observed for hypertrophy, indicating that higher training volumes enhance adaptations.While higher inertias favor eccentric power, the negative interaction between weekly volume and inertia suggests that higher volumes may attenuate the benefits of higher inertial loads.Training and testing specificity are crucial, as bilateral flywheel exercises produce greater overall strength adaptations than unilateral or mixed-limb configurations, and flywheel-based assessments more accurately capture concentric power adaptations than traditional testing methods.Athletes with higher training levels experienced greater improvements in concentric power than less trained athletes. Protocol registration:The original protocol was prospectively registered at the Open Science Framework: osf.io/s3qgr. Key Points Flywheel training improves strength and power, with bilateral exercises producing greater strength improvements than unilateral or mixed-limb approaches. Concentric power improvements are greater in highly trained athletes and are best assessed using flywheel-specific tests.Higher inertia enhances eccentric power, although high inertia combined with high training volume is counterproductive. Flywheel training yields small effects on hypertrophy and athletic performance.Hypertrophy follows a dose-response relationship, whereas improvements in athletic tasks are limited and not volumedependent, suggesting lower volumes are sufficient.
